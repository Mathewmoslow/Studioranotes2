Heart Failure

Overview

Heart failure (HF) is a complex clinical syndrome resulting from structural or functional impairment of ventricular filling or ejection of blood. It represents the final common pathway of various cardiac diseases and remains a leading cause of morbidity and mortality worldwide.

Key Takeaways

  • Definition: Heart cannot pump enough blood to meet metabolic demands
  • Types: Left-sided (systolic/diastolic), Right-sided, Biventricular
  • Classifications: NYHA Class I-IV based on functional limitation
  • Primary Goal: Improve quality of life and reduce hospitalizations

Pathophysiology

Left-Sided Heart Failure

The left ventricle fails to effectively pump blood into systemic circulation, leading to:

  1. Decreased cardiac output causing tissue hypoperfusion
  2. Backup of blood into pulmonary circulation
  3. Pulmonary congestion and edema
  4. Activation of compensatory mechanisms (RAAS, SNS)
"Forward failure" refers to inadequate cardiac output, while "backward failure" describes venous congestion from blood backing up behind the failing ventricle.

Right-Sided Heart Failure

Often develops secondary to left-sided failure. The right ventricle cannot adequately empty, causing systemic venous congestion manifesting as:

  • Peripheral edema (dependent areas)
  • Hepatomegaly and ascites
  • Jugular venous distension
  • Weight gain from fluid retention

Clinical Manifestations

Left-Sided HF Right-Sided HF
Dyspnea (exertional, orthopnea, PND) Peripheral edema
Pulmonary crackles Jugular venous distension
Cough (dry or frothy) Hepatomegaly
Fatigue and weakness Ascites
S3 heart sound (ventricular gallop) Weight gain

Nursing Interventions

Assessment Priorities

  • Daily weights (same time, same scale, same clothing)
  • Intake and output monitoring
  • Lung sounds assessment q4h
  • Peripheral edema assessment (pitting scale 1-4+)
  • Vital signs with oxygen saturation

Key Interventions

  1. Position: High Fowler's to reduce venous return and improve breathing
  2. Oxygen: Maintain SpO2 > 94% as ordered
  3. Fluid restriction: Typically 1.5-2L/day
  4. Sodium restriction: < 2g/day
  5. Activity: Balance rest with progressive activity

Practice Questions

1. A patient with heart failure reports waking up at night gasping for air. This symptom is known as:

A) Orthopnea
B) Paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea
C) Cheyne-Stokes respirations
D) Sleep apnea

Answer: B. Paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea (PND) is sudden awakening with severe shortness of breath, typically occurring 2-3 hours after falling asleep.

2. Which finding indicates worsening heart failure?

A) Weight loss of 1 lb
B) Weight gain of 3 lbs overnight
C) Increased urine output
D) Decreased BNP levels

Answer: B. Rapid weight gain (> 2-3 lbs in 24-48 hours) indicates fluid retention and worsening heart failure.
