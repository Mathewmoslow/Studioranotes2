Heart Failure

Overview

Heart failure (HF) is a complex clinical syndrome resulting from structural or functional impairment of ventricular filling or ejection of blood. It represents the final common pathway of various cardiac diseases and remains a leading cause of morbidity and mortality worldwide.

Key Point

Heart failure is NOT the same as a heart attack. HF is a chronic condition where the heart cannot pump effectively, while MI is acute ischemic injury to heart muscle.

Key Takeaways

  • Definition: Heart cannot pump enough blood to meet metabolic demands
  • Types: Left-sided (systolic/diastolic), Right-sided, Biventricular
  • Classifications: NYHA Class I-IV based on functional limitation
  • Primary Goal: Improve quality of life and reduce hospitalizations

Pathophysiology

Left-Sided Heart Failure

The left ventricle fails to effectively pump blood into systemic circulation, leading to:

  1. Decreased cardiac output causing tissue hypoperfusion
  2. Backup of blood into pulmonary circulation
  3. Pulmonary congestion and edema
  4. Activation of compensatory mechanisms (RAAS, SNS)

Clinical Point

Forward vs Backward Failure:

  • Forward failure: Inadequate cardiac output → tissue hypoperfusion, fatigue, weakness
  • Backward failure: Venous congestion → pulmonary edema (left) or peripheral edema (right)

Right-Sided Heart Failure

Often develops secondary to left-sided failure. The right ventricle cannot adequately empty, causing systemic venous congestion manifesting as:

  • Peripheral edema (dependent areas)
  • Hepatomegaly and ascites
  • Jugular venous distension
  • Weight gain from fluid retention

Clinical Manifestations

Left-Sided HF Right-Sided HF
Dyspnea (exertional, orthopnea, PND) Peripheral edema
Pulmonary crackles Jugular venous distension
Cough (dry or frothy) Hepatomegaly
Fatigue and weakness Ascites
S3 heart sound (ventricular gallop) Weight gain

Nursing Interventions

Nursing Focus: Assessment Priorities

  • Daily weights: Same time, same scale, same clothing
  • I&O: Strict monitoring, report imbalances
  • Lung sounds: Assess q4h for crackles
  • Edema: Pitting scale 1-4+, measure abdominal girth
  • Vitals: Include SpO2, orthostatic BP if applicable

Key Interventions

  1. Position: High Fowler's to reduce venous return and improve breathing
  2. Oxygen: Maintain SpO2 > 94% as ordered
  3. Fluid restriction: Typically 1.5-2L/day
  4. Sodium restriction: < 2g/day
  5. Activity: Balance rest with progressive activity

Medications

Medication: ACE Inhibitors (Lisinopril, Enalapril)

Mechanism: Block conversion of angiotensin I to angiotensin II, reducing afterload and preventing cardiac remodeling

Nursing Considerations:

  • Monitor for first-dose hypotension
  • Check potassium levels (risk of hyperkalemia)
  • Assess for dry cough (common side effect)
  • Hold if SBP < 90 mmHg

Medication: Loop Diuretics (Furosemide)

Mechanism: Block sodium and chloride reabsorption in the loop of Henle, promoting diuresis

Nursing Considerations:

  • Monitor daily weights and I&O
  • Check potassium (risk of hypokalemia)
  • Administer in morning to prevent nocturia
  • Monitor for ototoxicity with high doses

Patient Education

Patient Education: Self-Monitoring at Home

  • Daily weights: Weigh every morning after voiding, before eating. Report gain of > 2 lbs in 24 hours or > 5 lbs in a week
  • Sodium intake: Read nutrition labels, avoid processed foods, don't add salt at the table
  • Fluid intake: Follow prescribed restriction, use smaller cups, suck on ice chips
  • Activity: Stay as active as tolerated, rest when tired, elevate legs when sitting
  • Medications: Take as prescribed, never stop suddenly, know side effects to report

Practice Questions

1. A patient with heart failure reports waking up at night gasping for air. This symptom is known as:

A) Orthopnea
B) Paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea
C) Cheyne-Stokes respirations
D) Sleep apnea

Answer: B. Paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea (PND) is sudden awakening with severe shortness of breath, typically occurring 2-3 hours after falling asleep. This happens because fluid redistributes to the lungs when lying flat.

2. Which finding indicates worsening heart failure?

A) Weight loss of 1 lb
B) Weight gain of 3 lbs overnight
C) Increased urine output
D) Decreased BNP levels

Answer: B. Rapid weight gain (> 2-3 lbs in 24-48 hours) indicates fluid retention and worsening heart failure. This is why daily weights are critical for monitoring.
